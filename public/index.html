Software in the AI era

Treehouses and office buildings.

AI has made some software nearly free. If you need a treehouse, you can just about vibe-code it yourself this afternoon. You should.

An office building is a different job. You still want an architect, an engineer, a contractor, and yes, the permits. Data and software work the same way: prototypes and one-off scripts are cheaper than ever, but data infrastructure you can trust, and systems that scale, stay easy to maintain and extend, and hold up under real use, still take full-time thought and full-time craft.

A lot of software engineering now is knowing which job is in front of you. My work is to tell you honestly which one you have, help you vibe-code the treehouses, and build the office buildings properly.

You stay the domain expert. Let me be the software expert, one who can probably speak your domain too.

How I work

Use what you have. Build what you need.

Every engagement starts with the same question: does this call for the tool at hand, or a better one? Getting that answer right, over and over, at every scale, is most of the craft.

01

Listen for the bell

Start with what already exists. Sometimes the right answer is a better spreadsheet, a cleaned-up process, or one honest conversation about the metric, not a new system.

02

Follow the blackbird

When something new is called for, build it: small, sharp, and shippable in days to weeks. Modern cloud, lightweight tooling, and AI make this faster than it has ever been.

03

Leave it singing

Every tool ships with documentation and a handoff so your team owns it outright. You should depend on your software, never on your consultant.

The name

The bell, or the blackbird?

The sound of a bell
still reverberating,
or a blackbird calling
from the corner of a field.
Asking you to wake
into this life
or inviting you deeper
into the one that waits.
— David Whyte, “The Bell and the Blackbird”

The poem is about a choice we keep making: to live in the world as it is, or to build the one that could be. Both callings are true, and both take courage.

The same choice runs through this work. With every problem, every feature, every query, every line of code, you can reach for the tool at hand or craft one that fits better. Knowing which call to answer, decision by decision, is the work of a lifetime.

That tension is the practice. Hence the name.
